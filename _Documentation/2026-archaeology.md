# 2026 archaeology of *Time that land forgot*

A complete account of the May 2026 recreation of a 2004 Flash piece by
Timo Arnall and Even Westvang. What existed on disk, what Timo asked
for, what Claude found, what was preserved as bug, what was ported
verbatim, what was approximated, what was missed, where the human was
load-bearing.

Written 2026-05-09 from the working state of `/Volumes/Groke/Projects/2004 - Timeland/`,
the git history at commit `d008383`, the verbatim session transcript,
the original 2004 elasticspace blog post recovered from the WordPress
export at `/Volumes/Groke/Projects/2026 - Elasticspace update/00-source-of-truth/original-wp-export.xml`,
the FFDec-decompiled AS2 source at `/tmp/ffdec_out/scripts/frame_1/`,
and `_archive_2004/swfdump_full.txt` (the bytecode disassembly).

This is the technical record. The companion essay at
`01-content/drafts/draft-timeland-archaeology-2026.html` in the
elasticspace project is the lay version for publication.

## II. The 2004 design intent, in Timo and Even's words

The original blog post was published 30 July 2004 at 13:17 UTC at
<https://www.elasticspace.com/2004/07/timeland>. It has been preserved
verbatim in the WordPress XML export. The load-bearing passages:

> *We chose to create a balance of representation in the interface
> around a set of prerogatives: first image (for expressivity), then
> time (for narrative), then location (for spatialising, and
> commenting on, image and time).*

> *The first prototype is a linear experience of a journey, suitable
> for a gallery or screening, where images are overlaid into textural
> clusters of experience. It shows a scaling representation of the
> travel route based on the distance covered the last 20-30 minutes.
> Images recede in scale and importance as they move back in time.
> Each tick represents 1 minute, every red tick represents an hour.*

> *This was solved by creating a 'camera' connected to a spring
> system that attempts to center the image on the advancing 'now'
> while keeping a recent history of 20 points in view. The parser
> for the GPS tracks discards the positional data between the
> minutes and the animation is driven forward by every new 'minute'
> we find in the track and that is inserted into the view of the
> camera.*

> *There will be discontinuities in the tracklogs as the GPS is
> switched off during standstill and nights. Currently the system
> smoothes tracklog time to make breaks seem more like quick
> transitions.*

> *The system should ideally maintain a 'subjective feeling' of time
> adjusted to picture taking and movement; a temporal scaling as
> well as a spatial scaling. This would be an analog to our own
> remembering of events: minute memories from double loop
> roller-coasters, smudged holes of memory from sleepy nights.*

> *Most of the tweaking in the animation system went into refining
> the extents system around the camera history & zoom, acceleration
> and friction of spring systems and the ratio between insertion of
> new points and animation ticks.*

The post also documents that Timo had been photographing daily life
with a small digital camera for five years before the workshop, with
~7000 photos in the archive. The Iceland trip was a particular case
of an existing practice. The piece grew out of his "interest in the
narrative beyond the photographic frame" and Even's prior work on
"dynamic continuous interfaces" at polarfront.org.

The workshop was *Iceland inside and out*, hosted by Pallit at the
Iceland Academy of the Arts in H├Čfn. Matias Arje was also there,
working on related locative interfaces.

The closing thought of the 2004 post, which is worth recovering for
context:

> *Another strand of ideas we explored was using the metaphor of a
> 16mm Steenbeck edit deck: scrubbing 16mm film through the playhead
> and watching the resulting sound and image come together: we could
> use the scrubbing of an image timeline, to control all of the
> other metadata, and give real control to the user.*

The 2026 recreation has a scrub bar. The original Flash piece did
not. The scrub bar fulfils the wish in the closing paragraph of the
2004 post, twenty-two years after it was written.

## V. SWF mechanism reference

The original program structure, recoverable in full from the FFDec
decompile. Names and constants are quoted verbatim from AS2.

### V.1 Initialization order (DoAction_16.as)

On frame 1, `init()` runs:

1. Set Stage: `scaleMode = "noScale"`, `align = "TL"`.
2. Read URL parameters: `localData`, `noimages`, `DEBUG`. Set
   `displayImages = true` unless `?noimages=1`.
3. Pick URLs from one of three sets (local / elasticspace / debug).
   The shipped URLs were `http://www.elasticspace.com/timeland/timo_iceland_noflight.gpx`,
   `imageData.xml`, and `loc/`.
4. `gLatScale = -800`, `gLonScale = 400`.
5. Construct `_root.trackKeeper = new TrackKeeper()`,
   `cameraKeeper = new CameraKeeper()`,
   `imageKeeper = new ImageKeeper()`,
   `timeKeeper = new TimeKeeper()`.
6. `animationManager_addListener(cameraKeeper)`,
   `animationManager_addListener(timeKeeper)`.
7. Register Stage resize listener.
8. Construct AnimParser for the image XML (`imageBuilder` /
   `imageFinalizer`).
9. Construct AnimParser for the GPX (`trackBuilder` / `trackFinalizer`).
10. The animation loop runs forever from sprite 0022 at the SWF's
    declared 120 fps (actual on 2004 hardware: ~30 fps).

### V.2 The animation manager (DoAction_13.as)

Three functions plus an init:

```
function animationManager_init() {
   _root.listeners = new Array();
}
function animationManager_tick() {
   for (i in _root.listeners) {
      _root.listeners[i].animate();
   }
}
function animationManager_addListener(ref) {
   _root.listeners.push(ref);
}
function animationManager_removeListener(ref) {
   _root.listeners = removeElement(_root.listeners, ref);
}
```

Listener order at runtime (the order matters):
TimeKeeper, CameraKeeper, AnimParser (├Ś2 ŌĆö image XML, then GPX),
each Image as it gets associated with a trkpt.

Also defined in DoAction_13.as: an empty `function cameraManager() {}`
that is never called. Dead code that shipped.

### V.3 TimeKeeper (DoAction_2.as)

Fields: `time` (Date), `timeDest` (Date), `timeD` (number),
`medianTime` (unused). Methods: `setTime`, `getTime`, `reset`,
`animate`. The animate body is the load-bearing four-line spring:

```
TimeKeeper.prototype.animate = function() {
   t = this.time.getTime();
   tDest = this.timeDest.getTime();
   this.timeD += (t - tDest) * 4;
   this.timeD *= 0.1;
   if (this.timeD == nan) this.timeD = 0;
   this.time.setTime(this.time.getTime() - this.timeD);
};
```

Second-order damped spring. Stiffness 4├Ś, damping 0.1 per frame.
Steady-state lag at SWF's per-frame 14-second target advance:
~17.5 trip-seconds. The bug-for-bug reproduction in JS preserves the
`isNaN` guard as a single-line check.

### V.4 CameraKeeper (DoAction_4.as)

Three states defined: `CAMDRAWING = 0`, `LOCALTIME = 1`, `EXTENTS = 2`.
LOCALTIME is defined and never used in the shipped flow.

Fields per state:
- `currentX`, `currentY`, `dX`, `dY`, `destX`, `destY` ŌĆö the centre
  point and its velocity (CAMDRAWING).
- `currentX1..2`, `currentY1..2`, `dX1..2`, `dY1..2`,
  `destX1..2`, `destY1..2` ŌĆö the bounding box and its velocity
  (used by EXTENTS, also by CAMDRAWING for trail-window framing).

Spring constants per state:
- CAMDRAWING: `vel += err / 40; vel *= 0.69`
- EXTENTS:    `vel += err / 3;  vel *= 0.59`

Trail window in CAMDRAWING: `refocus(this, trackKeeper.minutes.slice(-15, -1))`,
i.e. the last 14 of 15 minutes. Last minute itself is the centre target.

Scale calculation:
```
case CAMDRAWING: scale = 90  * (mapWidth / (currentX2 - currentX1));
case EXTENTS:    scale = 100 * (mapWidth / (currentX2 - currentX1));
if (scale > 2500) scale = 2500;
canvas._xscale = scale; canvas._yscale = scale;
```

The 90├Ś / 100├Ś difference between states is the SWF v19 polish-pass
value. The 2500 cap is hard.

`buildFinished()` (called from `trackFinalizer`): switches state to
EXTENTS at end of trip parsing. This is what produces the final
zoom-out.

`refocus()` has a known scope leak: `x1`, `y1`, `x2`, `y2`, `i`, `x`,
`y` are all declared without `var`, so they leak as globals. Harmless
but characteristic of 2004 ActionScript.

### V.5 TrackKeeper (DoAction_5.as)

Fields: `points` (TrackPoints), `days`, `hours`, `minutes` (arrays
of marker objects), `lastDay`, `lastHour`, `lastMinute`, plus
`minLat`, `maxLat`, `minLon`, `maxLon` (the EXTENTS bounds).

`addPoint(lon, lat, elevation, time)` does five things in order:

1. `_root.timeKeeper.setTime(time)` ŌĆö the parser drives the clock.
2. Update display text:
   ```
   _root.display._displayTime = time.toString();
   _root.display._long = lon / gLatScale + "N";    // BUG (see VII)
   _root.display._lat  = lat / gLatScale + "W";    // BUG
   _root.display._elevation = "elevation:" + elevation + "m";
   ```
3. Construct `ref = new TrackPoint(lon, lat, elevation, time)`,
   push to `points`.
4. `updateExtents(lon, lat)`.
5. If `imageKeeper.getCurrentImageTime() < ref.getTime()`,
   `imageKeeper.associateCurrentImage(ref)` ŌĆö this is what activates
   the next photo. Note: triggered on parser-time, not on the eased
   display time. (See VI.7.)

Then minute-marker rule: `if (lastMinute == -1)` add first minute,
else `if (ref.getTime() > lastMinute.getTime() + 30000)` add new
minute. Then hour rule: `if (lastHour == -1)` first hour, else
`if (lastHour.getHours() != ref.getHours())` new hour.

`addDay()` exists but is never called in the shipped flow.

### V.6 ImageKeeper and Image (DoAction_3.as, DoAction_6.as)

ImageKeeper holds an array of Image objects and an index
`currentImage`. `addImage(time, fileName)` constructs and appends.
`getCurrentImageTime()` returns the time of the next-up image.
`associateCurrentImage(ref)` calls `setPointReference(ref)` on the
next image, advances `currentImage`, and registers the image as an
animation listener so its `animate()` will run every frame from now.

`Image.setPointReference(ref)`:
- Stores the trkpt.
- `attachMovieClip(_root.canvas.innerCanvas, "image")` ŌĆö attaches the
  sprite id 0014.
- Sets `clip.placeHolder._visible = false`, `_alpha = 0`, `_x = -100000`
  (off-screen during load).
- Sets `clip.placeholder.onCompleted = function() { this._parent.controller.timer = 0; this._parent.controller.loadFinished = 1; }`.
- `clip.placeholder.loadMovie(_root.ImagePath + this.fileName, "")`.
- Computes initial position: `x = point.getLongitude(); y = point.getLatitude();`
- Calls `scale(1)`, `updatePosition()`, `animate()`.

`Image.animate()`:
```
if (this.loadFinished) {
   this.timer += 1.2;
   if (this.timer > 3) {
      this.clip.placeholder._x = -160;
      this.clip.placeholder._y = -106.5;
      this.clip.placeholder._visible = true;
   }
   this.clip.placeholder._alpha = this.timer;
   if (this.timer > 100) {
      trace("fade finished");
      this.loadfinished = 0;     // BUG ŌĆö wrong case (see VII)
   }
}
if (this.hasRef) {
   this.scale(3.5 + 9000000 / (Math.abs(_root.timeKeeper.getTime() - this.time.getTime()) + 1));
}
```

Two notes:

- The fade ramp: `timer += 1.2` per frame, `_alpha = timer`. Reaches
  100 in 83 frames, which at 30 fps real is 2.77 seconds.
- The scale formula: `3.5 + 9_000_000 / (|now - photoTime|_ms + 1)`.
  At `dt = 0` this is 9,000,003.5 (absurd; Flash clamps via the canvas
  scale cap, the JS port via a `MAX_FINAL_SCALE = 100` safety). At
  `dt = 1000 ms` (1 trip-second), 9,003.5. At `dt = 9_000_000 ms`
  (2.5 trip-hours), 4.5. The function shrinks fast then long-tails.

### V.7 AnimParser (DoAction_14.as)

Wraps an XML object. The constructor calls `XMLDoc.load(fileName)`
and registers itself as an animation listener. Its `animate()`:

```
if (this.XMLDoc.loaded) {
   cnt = 0;
   while (cnt < 1) {
      name = this.XMLDoc.parseNext();
      if (this.XMLDoc.doneParsing) {
         this.removeFromAnimationListener();
      }
      cnt++;
   }
}
```

One node per animate frame. **This is the load-bearing fact of the
piece's pacing.** When the GPS sampled densely (stationary,
1ŌĆō2 s apart), the parser dwelled. When sparsely (fast motion,
10ŌĆō30 s apart), the parser raced. The piece's rhythm is the GPS's
own attention rhythm, not a clock.

### V.8 dateConvert (DoAction_12.as)

```
function dateConvert(time) {
   t = new Date();
   t.setYear(time.slice(0, 4));
   t.setDate(time.slice(8, 10));
   t.setHours(time.slice(11, 13));      // local TZ! see VII
   t.setMinutes(time.slice(14, 16));
   t.setSeconds(time.slice(17, 19));
   t.setMonth(time.slice(5, 7) - 1);    // last (per the changelog fix)
   return t;
}
```

`setHours()` in Flash 6 sets the local-timezone hour. The "Z" suffix
on the GPX trkpt timestamp is silently dropped. On Even's CEST-set
machine, every UTC trkpt was reinterpreted as local CEST. The setMonth
ordering is the one the 31 July 2004 changelog records as fixed.

## VI. The port

The recreation is `index.html` at the project root. Single file,
~735 lines, vanilla JavaScript with `<canvas>`. No build, no
dependencies. What it preserves:

### VI.1 Constants

Every numeric in the SWF traced in. The port adds four constants
the SWF didn't have: `MAX_FINAL_SCALE = 80 ├Ś (SCALE_CAP/2500)` (a
safety cap on the bloom that auto-tracks `SCALE_CAP`; see ┬¦VI.6 and
┬¦X.11), `PLAY_IN = 90` (frames of pre-rolled physics for the
`?seek=` URL parameter), `SWF_FPS = 30` (empirical; the SWF header
lies), and a `SCALE_CAP` override of 3800 vs the v19 literal 2500
(calibrated against the .mov; see ┬¦X.11).

### VI.2 Spring math, ported verbatim

```js
// CAMDRAWING ŌĆö DoAction_4.as
cam.vel.x += (cam.dest.x - cam.cur.x) * SPRING_K_CAM;   // / 40
cam.vel.x *= SPRING_DAMP_CAM;                           // 0.69
cam.cur.x += cam.vel.x;
```

```js
// TimeKeeper ŌĆö DoAction_2.as
timeD += (dispT - targetT) * 4;
timeD *= 0.1;
if (isNaN(timeD)) timeD = 0;
dispT -= timeD;
```

### VI.3 Physics tick order, ported verbatim

The SWF's `animationManager_tick` calls listeners in registration
order. The JS replicates exactly:

```js
while (physicsAccum >= SWF_FRAME_MS && physicsTicks < 8) {
  physicsAccum -= SWF_FRAME_MS;
  // (1) parser advances 1 trkpt
  if (playing && parserIdx < allPts.length - 1) {
    parserIdx += 1;
    setT(allPts[parserIdx].t);
  }
  // (2) TimeKeeper damped spring
  timeD += (dispT - targetT) * 4;
  timeD *= 0.1;
  dispT -= timeD;
  // (3) Camera animate
  camera.state = (parserIdx >= allPts.length - 1) ? 'EXTENTS' : 'CAMDRAWING';
  cameraAnimate(camera, activeNow, bounds, SWF_W);
  // (4) Photo timers (parser-time triggered)
  for (const p of photos) {
    if (targetT >= p.t) p.timer = Math.min(100, p.timer + FADE_PER_SWF_FRAME);
    else p.timer = 0;
  }
}
```

### VI.4 Hairline strokes

The SWF stores stroke widths in twips (0.05 px = 1 twip = hairline).
Flash renders hairlines as 1 screen pixel regardless of zoom. Canvas
2D has no hairline; the JS replicates by setting `lineWidth = 1 / current_transform_scale`,
recomputing per draw call.

### VI.5 Z-order

Photos are drawn first, then minutes, then hours. This matches the
SWF's `clipIterator` depth ordering: in `addPoint`, an Image is
attached before its Minute and Hour, so markers end up on higher
depths and render on top. Required correction during the session
once Timo noticed photos were sitting on top of markers in the
recreation.

### VI.6 The bloom math

```js
let photoScale = (3.5 + 9_000_000 / (dt + 1)) / 100;
if (photoScale * k > MAX_FINAL_SCALE) photoScale = MAX_FINAL_SCALE / k;
```

`/ 100` because the SWF stored scale as a percent (the JS canvas
transform expects a multiplier). The safety cap protects
`drawImage` from absurd first-frame sizes; the natural curve at
SWF's 2500 scale cap shrinks rapidly enough that it's invisible
otherwise.

### VI.7 What the port adds that the SWF didn't have

- **A scrub bar.** The 2004 piece played start to end. The 2004 blog
  post itself wished for this in its closing paragraph (the Steenbeck
  reference). The JS adds a scrub control with `lastIndexBefore`
  searching for time lookup and `parserIdx` tracking in sync.
- **`?seek=` and `?play=` URL parameters.** Used for headless screenshots
  during the side-by-side video work. `?seek=N` jumps to a fraction
  of the trip and runs `PLAY_IN = 90` frames of simulated playback
  through the target so the camera spring is mid-transient at the
  rendered moment, not fully converged.
- **Hi-DPI canvas.** A `devicePixelRatio` multiplier so the work is
  crisp on retina displays.
- **`SWF_FPS = 30` empirical override.** The header declares 120; the
  measured rate from the .mov is ~30.6.
- **Responsive container fill.** The 500 ├Ś 500 SWF design size is
  preserved as the internal coordinate system, but the rendered
  canvas now fills its CSS container at any width. A `ResizeObserver`
  syncs the drawing buffer to `clientWidth ├Ś devicePixelRatio` on
  every resize so the artefact stays sharp on hosts wider than 500
  px. SWF design units map to drawing-buffer pixels via a single
  `canvasScale = buf / SWF_W` factor that replaces the old hard-coded
  DPR multiply.
- **Iframe-host-aware styling.** The body background is transparent
  so whichever host page (light or dark) shows through; the artefact
  canvas keeps its `#f9f8f6` warm-grey; play button and scrub bar use
  translucent greys (`rgba(128,128,128,0.20)` to `0.32`) that read
  on either a light or a dark host. A `?bg=white` URL parameter
  forces a white canvas background for headless screen-recording
  fairness (see ┬¦XV).
- **3 px stage corner rounding.** A small `border-radius: 3px` on
  the stage and canvas softens the squareness without making the
  artefact read as a rounded UI element.

### VI.8 What the SWF had that the port doesn't

- **LOCALTIME camera state.** Defined but never used at runtime. Not
  ported; pointless.
- **Day primitive and `addDay()`.** Constructor exists, never called.
  Not ported.
- **`?noimages` mode.** A degraded text-only version for slow Flash
  plugins. Not ported (the photos load fine in 2026).
- **`?nocache=` cache busting.** Not ported (HTTP caching is
  controlled by headers in 2026).
- **The empty `cameraManager()` function.** Dead code that shipped.
  Not worth porting.

## VII. Bugs preserved on purpose (and the two later reverted)

Six bugs in the original code were preserved in the port for archival
fidelity. Each is documented in the `index.html` comments so the next
reader doesn't fix them.

On 2026-05-09 (after the writeup first posted), two of the six were
reverted: VII.1 (the lat/lon scale swap) and VII.2 (the GMT+0200
timezone label). Reasons and principle in ┬¦VII.7. Both reverts now
shipped in `index.html`. Sections VII.1 and VII.2 below preserve the
original analysis and add the post-revert state at the end of each.

### VII.1 The lat/lon scale swap in display

```as2
// DoAction_5.as:24-25 (verbatim)
_root.display._long = lon / gLatScale + "N";
_root.display._lat  = lat / gLatScale + "W";
```

Both lines divide by `gLatScale` (ŌłÆ800). The `_long` line should
divide by `gLonScale` (400). The result: longitude displays as
`lon / -800` instead of `lon / 400`, producing a value of constant
ratio ŌłÆ0.5 vs the true longitude. For Iceland (~ŌłÆ21┬░W), the displayed
value is ~10.5┬░, labelled "W" ŌĆö a "mid-Atlantic" reading.

The label is also swapped between fields: `_long` gets "N" (a
latitude cardinal); `_lat` gets "W" (a longitude cardinal). Two bugs
on top of each other.

The JS originally preserved both:

```js
ctx.fillText(trkpt.raw_lat.toFixed(6) + 'N', 14, 25);     // position of _long
ctx.fillText((-trkpt.raw_lon).toFixed(6) + 'W', 104, 25);  // position of _lat
```

**Reverted 2026-05-09.** The display now reads true latitude in the
"N" field and true longitude in the "W" field, so a viewer reading
the coordinates can map them to a real point in Iceland. The
displayed value used to be ~10.5 ┬░W (mid-Atlantic) regardless of
where the trip actually was; the corrected display reads ~21.9 ┬░W
in the Reykjav├Łk area, etc. See ┬¦VII.7 for the reversion criterion.

### VII.2 The "GMT+0200" label that's actually wrong

`dateConvert()` parses GPX `Z` strings as local CEST (Even's machine).
Then `Date.toString()` re-serialises with the player's TZ label, which
on Even's CEST machine printed "GMT+0200". The label has nothing to
do with Iceland (which is GMT, no DST). It's the SWF's bug printed
twice over.

The JS originally preserved the label format. The underlying time
variable is true UTC (verified by the Geysir test, see X.5).

```js
function fmtFlashDate(t) {
   const d = new Date(t);   // true UTC, no shift
   return DAYS[d.getUTCDay()] + ' ' + MONTHS[d.getUTCMonth()] + ' ' +
          pad2(d.getUTCDate()) + ' ' + pad2(d.getUTCHours()) + ':' +
          pad2(d.getUTCMinutes()) + ':' + pad2(d.getUTCSeconds()) +
          ' GMT+0200 ' + d.getUTCFullYear();
}
```

**Reverted 2026-05-09.** The label now reads the actual UTC time in
Iceland (which is on UTC year-round, no DST). The previous label
asserted CEST, two hours ahead of the trip's real local time; for
anyone reading the clock to follow the trip ŌĆö when did we leave
H├Čfn, when did we reach Reykjav├Łk ŌĆö the label was actively
misleading. The underlying time variable was always true UTC, so
the change is a label-format change only, not a recomputation:
`fmtFlashDate` now emits the suffix `GMT` instead of `GMT+0200`.
See ┬¦VII.7 for the reversion criterion.

### VII.3 The squashed projection

`lat * -800` and `lon * 400` is not a map projection. It is a 2:1
horizontal squash of latitude relative to longitude. At Iceland's
~64┬░N, a true Mercator projection would *expand* longitude (cos(64┬░) Ōēł 0.44),
not compress it. The piece's distinctive look (a tall, vertically
stretched Iceland) comes from this distortion.

JS preserves: `x: lon * G_LON_SCALE; y: lat * G_LAT_SCALE` (line
117ŌĆō118 of `index.html`).

### VII.4 The `loadfinished` typo

```as2
// DoAction_6.as:57 (verbatim)
_loc1_.loadfinished = 0;     // lowercase 'f'
```

The check at the top of the function uses the camelCase
`loadFinished`. The reset at line 57 creates a *new* property
`loadfinished` with a lowercase f. So the reset never actually
fires. The timer keeps incrementing past 100. Flash silently clamps
`_alpha` at 100, so the visible behaviour is correct, but the code
has a broken state machine.

The JS doesn't replicate this exactly (it uses the cap `Math.min(100,
p.timer + FADE_PER_SWF_FRAME)` directly), but the visible behaviour
is identical: photo timer reaches 100 and stays there.

### VII.5 The `dateConvert` setMonth-last ordering

```as2
// DoAction_12.as:5-10
t.setYear(time.slice(0, 4));
t.setDate(time.slice(8, 10));   // BEFORE setMonth
t.setHours(time.slice(11, 13));
t.setMinutes(time.slice(14, 16));
t.setSeconds(time.slice(17, 19));
t.setMonth(time.slice(5, 7) - 1);   // LAST
```

This is the bug the 31 July 2004 changelog records as fixed:
*"error in flash in date object, month must be set last"*. If you
call `setDate(31)` while the Date object is in February, the day
rolls over. The fix on the PC was to set month last, after day.

Preserved as the literal AS2 logic. The JS uses `Date.UTC()`
directly, which doesn't have the bug, but the AS2's order is
recorded in the comments.

### VII.6 The variable scope leak in `refocus`

```as2
// DoAction_4.as:114-140
CameraKeeper.prototype.refocus = function(points) {
   x1 = 0; y1 = 0; x2 = 0; y2 = 0;   // no var
   i = 0;                              // no var
   while (i < points.length) {
      x = points[i].getLongitude();   // no var
      y = points[i].getLatitude();    // no var
      ...
   }
};
```

Every variable leaks to the global scope. AS2 allowed this. Functionally
harmless because nothing else uses these names at the global level.
Characteristic of the era and worth recording.

The JS port uses `let` everywhere (no leak).

### VII.7 The reversion criterion: charm vs experience-cost

VII.1 and VII.2 were preserved on the same logic as the others ŌĆö
this is what the original showed; reproducing it is part of the
port's archival fidelity. After living with the live page for a few
days, Timo named the criterion that overruled that logic:

> The clock at the top reads two hours later than it should, because
> that is the way the original showed it. The longitude reads as a
> value somewhere in the middle of the Atlantic, because that is the
> way the original showed it. I would like to change this, to
> correct the bugs, to make it more real. Should bugs be preserved?
> If they are charming, but not if they detract from our ability to
> experience something in the data.

The remaining preserved bugs all sit on the *charm* side:

- **VII.3 squashed projection.** The 2:1 lat/lon ratio is the visual
  identity of the piece, the reason Iceland reads as a tall
  vertically-stretched form rather than a flat outline. Removing it
  produces a different artefact. Stays.
- **VII.4 `loadfinished` typo.** Has no visible effect (Flash clamps
  `_alpha` at 100). The typo is preserved as a code-comment record
  of the era; the JS doesn't have the bug to begin with. Stays.
- **VII.5 `dateConvert` setMonth-last ordering.** The comment in the
  changelog naming the fix is part of the artefact. JS uses
  `Date.UTC()` and doesn't have the bug. Stays.
- **VII.6 `refocus` scope leak.** Era-typical AS2 sloppiness, no
  visible effect. JS uses `let`. Stays.

VII.1 and VII.2 sat on the *experience-cost* side. Both are values
the viewer reads off the screen as part of *following the trip*: the
clock answers "what time is it on this trip?", the lat/lon answer
"where on the map are we?". Reading wrong values for both made the
piece less legible *as a record of Iceland in July 2004*, which is
what it is supposed to be a record of.

The criterion generalises. Every preserved-bug call in software
archaeology can be tested against: *does this bug add to the
character of the original, or does it obstruct the thing the
original was trying to communicate?* If character: keep it. If
obstruction: revert it, and note the revert in the documentation
(this section) and in the code (a comment at the call site). The
original analysis stays in ┬¦VII.1 / ┬¦VII.2 above so a future reader
can see the bug, see the call to preserve it, and see the call to
revert it, in that order.

The deeper point: archival fidelity to a piece of work is fidelity
to *what the work was trying to do*, not to every byte of how it
once did it. Bug-fidelity is a tactic, not the goal. When the
tactic and the goal point opposite directions, the goal wins.

## X. The audit chain

Each finding, with its source, the test that surfaced it, and the
fix.

### X.1 The 120 fps lie

- Found in: SWF header (`Frame rate: 120.000000`).
- Initial port took at face value.
- Disagreement: a 5-day trip at 120 trkpts/sec finishes in 49
  seconds; the .mov is 192 seconds.
- Test: count trkpts (5,869), divide by .mov runtime (192). Result:
  30.6 trkpts/sec.
- Confirmation: every spring constant in the file (`*= 0.69`,
  `*= 0.59`, `*= 0.1`) is critically damped at 30 Hz, overdamped at
  120 Hz. They were calibrated for the lower rate.
- Fix: `SWF_FPS = 30` with a fixed-timestep accumulator. Total
  playback 195 s (vs .mov's 192 s). Commit `94e6170`.

### X.2 The TimeKeeper damped spring

- Found in: `DoAction_2.as:23-35`.
- Initial port: first-order ease (`dispT += (targetT - dispT) * 0.35`).
- Disagreement: the .mov shows the camera carrying momentum and
  overshooting the target on settle; the first-order ease just
  glides in.
- Test: derive the steady-state lag of each. AS2 spring lags 17.5
  trip-seconds at the SWF's per-frame trkpt advance; first-order
  ease lags ~5 seconds.
- Fix: port the AS2 spring verbatim, including the `isNaN` guard.
  Commit `94e6170`.

### X.3 The CXForm `a=0.1` rabbit hole

The longest thread of the session. The placeholder layer (sprite
0013 inside sprite 0014) carries a CXForm `mul a=0.1` in its
placement matrix. Read literally, this caps every photo at 10%
alpha.

The cap was changed seven times before settling:

| Pass | Cap | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 0.10 | Literal CXForm reading. Photos almost invisible. |
| 2 | 0.35 | Compromise. |
| 3 | 0.55 | "Layered see-through where they stack." |
| 4 | 0.85 | Re-reading `loadMovie` semantics. |
| 5 | 0.30 | Tighter for overlap translucency. |
| 6 | 0.20 | "Closer to .mov perceived alpha." |
| 7 | 1.00 | Final. The CXForm was on the *empty* placeholder; `loadMovie` replaces the clip's contents and (per Flash 6 behaviour) does not preserve the placement transform. So at `timer=100` the photo is fully opaque. |

The layered see-through effect in the .mov is *size disparity*, not
transparency: new photos bloom huge, old ones shrink to small frames
behind. Stacks read as translucent because each layer is small and
opaque against the opaque layer beneath. Commit `d3e1719`.

### X.4 The TZ shift attempt and reversal

- Symptom: .mov clock labels are 2 hours earlier than corresponding
  GPX UTC timestamps.
- First hypothesis: shift both EXIF and GPX by ŌłÆ2 hours so the
  displayed clock matches the .mov.
- Timo's correction: *"I'm sure the camera was set exactly to local
  time, that can be a source of truth."*
- Resolution: the .mov's labels are the SWF's bug, not the truth.
  Camera was UTC. Display label kept matching .mov format ("GMT+0200")
  for side-by-side comparison; underlying time variable is true UTC.
  Commit `f7d73f8`.

### X.5 The Geysir empirical test

The conclusive test for camera time origin. Process:

- Detect every long pause in the GPX (camera stationary, tightly
  clustered trkpts). 55 pauses found at >5 minutes.
- One pause is unmistakable: Sat 3 July 2004, 12:30ŌĆō13:02 UTC, lat
  64.31313, lon ŌłÆ20.29741. Geysir, the original geyser.
- Find photos with EXIF stamps inside that window under each TZ
  hypothesis. Look at what they show.
- Under EXIF-as-UTC: 5 photos in window. All Geysir-content (warning
  sign, person crouching by steaming pool, tourists with cameras,
  steaming geothermal pool with calcified rock). Photo IDs:
  DSC02713, DSC02732, DSC02736, DSC02748.
- Under EXIF +2h: 153 photos before GPX even starts; median speed
  for in-range photos is 5 km/h (i.e. many appear to be from moving
  vehicles, implausible for composed shots).

Camera was UTC. Confirmed. The wider statistical check: 144 of 241
photos at walking/still speed (median 1.9 km/h) under EXIF-as-UTC.

This is the kind of test Claude proposes and executes (pause
detection, intersection, statistics). The recognition step ("that is
Geysir") needs Timo or someone with geographic priors.

### X.6 The lat/lon swap, by map-shape audit

- Symptom: .mov displays "10.95W" longitude at a moment when GPX
  shows ŌłÆ21.88.
- First hypothesis: .mov used a different (fuller) GPX containing the
  trans-Atlantic flight, hence "mid-Atlantic" longitudes.
- Test: compare lat and lon at the same instant. Latitudes are
  byte-identical. Longitudes are related by a constant: ŌłÆ21.88 ├Ś ŌłÆ0.5 = 10.94.
  Constant ratio means same data through a buggy formula, not
  different data.
- Bytecode confirms: `display._long = lon / gLatScale + "N"`.
  `gLatScale` is ŌłÆ800; `gLonScale` is 400. The division by the wrong
  scale produces the constant ratio.

No mythical fuller GPX. Same Iceland data, displayed wrong.

### X.7 Trkpt-paced playback (the "alive" unlock)

- Symptom: the recreation felt mechanical; the .mov "looks alive"
  during stops.
- Mechanism in AS2 (`AnimParser.animate`, `DoAction_14.as:15-30`):
  one trkpt per animate frame, regardless of timestamps. Pacing
  shaped entirely by GPS sampling density.
- Stops: GPS samples densely (every 1ŌĆō2 s), parser dwells, camera
  spring settles, photos bloom.
- Motion: GPS samples sparsely (every 10ŌĆō30 s), parser races,
  camera struggles to keep up, photos pass quickly.
- **The hidden second consequence: empty time disappears.** The 2004
  GPX has overnight gaps where the GPS was switched off. Walking the
  array, those gaps take zero frames; the parser just steps over
  them. The 2004 blog post named this directly: *"There will be
  discontinuities in the tracklogs as the GPS is switched off
  during standstill and nights. Currently the system smoothes
  tracklog time to make breaks seem more like quick transitions."*
  The first port advanced time linearly across the calendar, which
  meant overnight gaps became long boring stretches with photographs
  sitting unchanged for tens of seconds while nothing happened. The
  trkpt-paced advance fixed both problems with one move: stops
  dwell, empty time disappears.
- Initial port used a fixed linear advance (3 min for whole trip).
  Compressed Geysir's 32-minute stop into 1.5 real seconds and
  inflated overnight nothing into ten seconds of static screen.
- Fix: `parserIdx += 1` per physics tick. 5,869 trkpts / 30 fps =
  195 s, matches .mov's 192 s. Stops dwell, motion races, nights
  vanish. Commit `94e6170`.
- Surfaced after publication by Even Westvang reading the writeup:
  *"Interesting that Claude's first pass was just a very ordinary
  photo + gpx track rendering and had lots of long overnight
  pauses. Boring indeed."* The "boring overnight pauses" half is
  the half this section originally underplayed. Folded in here.

### X.8 Marker z-order

- Found by Timo's eye: *"looks to me like the circles and crosshairs
  are on top of the photos in the original."*
- Source in AS2: `TrackKeeper.addPoint` creates Image first, then
  Minute, then Hour. `clipIterator` depth ordering means later-attached
  clips have higher depths.
- First port drew photos last (on top). Fix: draw photos first,
  markers second.

### X.9 The seek-converge regression (and the bbox-snap fix that came after)

- Symptom: screenshot comparison videos showed the recreation
  artificially still at every captured frame, while the .mov captured
  dynamic mid-motion frames.
- Root cause: the `?seek=` parameter ran 200 ticks of camera spring
  physics to converge before rendering. Result: zero spring transient.
- Fix: replace converge with play-in. Seek lands 90 trkpts before
  target, settles photo timers at that earlier point, then simulates
  90 frames of real playback through the target. Camera spring
  mid-transient at the rendered moment. Commit `d008383`.

A second symptom emerged later, while sweeping `SCALE_CAP` across
the timeline (see ┬¦X.11): the seek-mode screenshots reported the
camera bbox as ~2.5├Ś wider than its destination value at the
rendered moment. The .mov, in continuous playback, had had minutes
of physics to settle the bbox spring; my 90-frame play-in could not
match that from a cold seek-jump start. So every screenshot
comparison until then had been measuring transient spring state, not
converged state, and any conclusion drawn about visual scale was
suspect. Fix: snap `cur` to `dest` at the end of the seek's
play-in (bbox + main camera + zero velocities). Live playback is
unaffected; only the headless screenshot path lands in a converged
state matching the .mov. Commit `cb90d7d`.

### X.10 Words that did unjustified work

- *"matches closely"*, *"broadly similar"*, *"roughly"* in Claude
  summaries.
- Timo's correction: *"these words are doing a lot of work."*
- Lesson for archaeology: only verifiable matches count. Either two
  frames are the same or they are not. Closely is doing work.

### X.11 SCALE_CAP recalibration (2500 ŌåÆ 3800)

- Symptom: the recreation felt more zoomed-out than the .mov during
  walking-speed phases. Markers and photographs read smaller than in
  the .mov panel of the side-by-side, especially in dense
  Reykjav├Łk/Geysir clusters.
- Diagnosis required X.9's bbox-snap fix first, otherwise every
  screenshot comparison was reading transient spring state and the
  symptom got mis-attributed.
- Method: parameter sweep across 8 timeline moments ├Ś 4 caps
  (2500/3200/3800/4500), captured headlessly with the bbox-snap
  applied. Walking-phase moments (m150 pegs, m185 tile, etc.) were
  the fair-comparison set; moments where the .mov was mid-TimeKeeper
  fast-forward were excluded because the panels disagreed on which
  moment they were rendering, not on scale.
- Result: cap = 3800 sat consistently closest to the .mov on the
  walking-phase set. cap = 2500 (v19's value) felt zoomed-out.
  cap = 4500 felt zoomed-in.
- Hypothesis: v19 lowered the cap from an earlier SWF iteration.
  The .mov was screen-recorded against an earlier SWF whose cap was
  closer to 3800. The disassembly we have is v19, which is why the
  literal value in ┬¦V.10 is 2500 and the port runs 3800.
- Bloom-cap follow-on: `MAX_FINAL_SCALE` is set as
  `80 ├Ś (SCALE_CAP / 2500)` so the bloomŌåÆsettle dynamic stays
  smooth at any cap (see ┬¦VI.6). At 3800 this resolves to ~122.
- Commit `cace6db`.

The SCALE_CAP=3800 is the second non-SWF-faithful constant in the
port, after the +15 % marker bump in ┬¦XV.5. Both are flagged in the
code as side-by-side rig calibrations rather than fidelity claims.

Six things:

1. **Decide what the work is.** "Tool" or "film". The choice
   reframed everything that followed. Not in any file.
2. **Decide what counts as character.** The lat/lon swap and the
   wrong clock label are bugs. Whether to fix them or preserve them
   is an authorial decision. Timo's instruction was "preserve". With

3. **Recognise Geysir from the photographs.** Five JPEGs of warning

5. **Push back on confident-sounding wrong readings.** "I'm sure
   the camera was set to local time." "Don't dodge the question."
   "These words are doing a lot of work." The push-back was
   load-bearing in at least three places.
6. **Bring memory of the trip.** "We stopped at a hot spring and

## XIII. Ten things to keep in mind for the next archaeology

1. The disassembler tool exists; use it before guessing.
2. A continuous video record of the original running is worth a
   thousand static screenshots.
3. Header values can lie. Measure the real one from the artefact.
4. CXForms and other "applied to the empty container" transforms
   are easy to misread. Read the runtime semantics, not just the
   placement structure. Try the literal reading and the runtime
   reading both, and compare each against the .mov.
5. Time zones in pre-IETF-era code were often parsed as local
   without notice. Don't trust the player's clock label.
6. Camera EXIF time settings are a separate question from the
   GPS UTC; never assume.
7. Use *content* in the photographs as ground-truth landmarks
   against the GPS (the Geysir test).
8. Pacing data structures by index rather than by time was once
   common. Don't resample on the way in; honour the original
   cadence.
9. Bugs preserved on purpose need a sentence of prose somewhere
   visible (README, page footer, code comment). Otherwise the next
   reader fixes them.
10. The author's recollection of *which moment felt right* is a
    primary source. Capture it in writing while there's still
    someone who remembers.

## XIV. What this archaeology actually was

It was not a port of one binary to another. It was a sequence of
hypotheses run against three corroborating sources ŌĆö the bytecode,
the screen-recording, and the author's memory ŌĆö where the author was
present and willing to push back. Every hypothesis that survived was
the one that satisfied all three. Every hypothesis that died, died
because two of the three disagreed.

The medium for the conversation was a terminal, a browser open to
localhost, and Timo at the keyboard. The medium for the work itself
remained what it always was: a 500 ├Ś 500 viewport in a browser, white
background, photographs blooming on a GPS trail in Iceland in July
2004. That part didn't change. Only the path back to it did.

The 2004 closing paragraph asked for a Steenbeck. The 2026 recreation
has a scrub bar. The wish in the original made it into the
restoration, twenty-two years later.

correction, iterate" stopped scaling. Timo's questions were getting
sharper than the available evidence. The fix was a side-by-side
comparison harness: render the .mov on the left, render the recreation
at the matching parser-time on the right, composite to MP4 and GIF,
look at frames side-by-side. Once the harness existed, every audit
collapsed from "judge by feel" to "look at the divergent pixels".

The remaining differences between v13's two panels are small in
pixel terms and load-bearing in experiential terms. They are not
"polish-level" residuals; they are the part of the work that decides
whether the recreation feels like the original or feels like a
diagram of it.

Photo lead/lag. The SWF used `loadMovie` async, so a photo's
appearance is gated by network/cache latency from 2004 ŌĆö the image
*arrives*, with the small flicker of arrival. The recreation
pre-caches everything and shows photos instantly on activation. In
pixel-diff terms this is a phase shift of fractions of a second; in
felt terms it removes the sense that the trip is being re-discovered
as it plays, and replaces it with the sense that everything is
pre-known. Open. Worth fixing by introducing a synthetic short delay
on activation, or by reading the SWF's actual load-order and
honouring it.

Filmstrip persistence. The camera centring decides whether older
photos stay in view as the trip moves on, or fall out. The SWF and
the recreation diverge at the edges by a few centring-pixels, which
in the dense Reykjav├Łk and Geysir clusters is the difference between
a photo persisting through five minutes of trip-time and being lost
in two. This is the heart of what Even called "textural clusters of
experience". Open.

End-of-trip drift. Safari hits ~25ŌĆō28 fps under live screen-recording
load, the SWF's effective rate is 30 fps, so the recording's
parser-time lags the .mov's by a few percent that compound over 192 s
into seconds of phase error at the end. The drift is a
recording-rig artefact, not a recreation bug ŌĆö the live page runs
at full rate. But it means the side-by-side at the back end of the
trip is not a fair comparison. A deterministic test-mode that ticks
both sources from the same wall-clock would dissolve it.

### XV.1 The OCR-driven sync technique

The seek-mapping bugs above all came from trying to compute the
recreation's seek position analytically. The reliable approach
(version 9) was to OCR the .mov's display label out of each frame and
build the seek manifest from the literal timestamps.

```bash
ffmpeg -i mov_frames/f${N}.jpg -vf "crop=300:50:30:0,scale=...,negate" - |
   tesseract - - --psm 6 -c tessedit_char_whitelist="0-9A-Za-z:+- "
```

OCR succeeded on 130 of 192 frames; the rest were interpolated between
adjacent successes. With this manifest, headless Chrome captured the
recreation at the exact same trip-time the .mov displayed. The two
panels were synced at the millisecond level.

### XV.2 The discovery of the registration-order divergence

Reading the AS2 init code more carefully (DoAction_16.as) revealed
that the SWF registers `cameraKeeper` *before* `timeKeeper` in the
animation listener list:

```
animationManager_addListener(_root.cameraKeeper);
animationManager_addListener(_root.timeKeeper);
```

So per tick, the SWF runs:
parser ŌåÆ cameraKeeper ŌåÆ timeKeeper ŌåÆ images.

Camera reads the *previous* frame's eased dispT, then TimeKeeper
updates dispT for the *next* frame to read. One-frame phase lag
across every frame.

The JS port had the order reversed (parser ŌåÆ timeKeeper ŌåÆ cameraKeeper
ŌåÆ images) which means camera reads the *current* frame's eased
dispT. The absolute lag is one frame (~33 ms at 30 fps), but it's a
phase between two coupled springs running at the same time, on the
same data ŌĆö exactly the relationship that produces the *settling*
the viewer reads as "alive". Reversing the registration order lets
camera and time act on the same frame instead of one trailing the
other, which subtly changes the chase behaviour at every transition
between motion and rest. Not yet fixed in `index.html`. Open.

### XV.3 From headless seek to live-recorded playback

By v9 the harness was as good as headless capture could make it. Each
right-panel frame was the recreation rendered at the exact trip-time
the .mov displayed, written out by Chromium with `?seek=0..1`. The
problem became visible the moment Timo asked about settling
behaviour: the right panel never showed any. Camera settle, photo
bloom decay, marker chase during fast motion ŌĆö every transient was
already gone by the time the seek-and-screenshot ran. We were
comparing the .mov's live evolution to the recreation's steady state.

The shift in v10 was to stop seeking and start recording. Position
Safari at the top-left of the screen, load the page with `?play=1` so
it auto-plays, run `ffmpeg avfoundation` for 210 s while the
recreation plays through. Then extract that recording at 1 fps,
OCR the displayed timestamp out of each frame, build a manifest of
"recording-frame N ŌåÆ trip-time T", and match each .mov frame's
displayed time to its nearest recording-frame time.

Two things fall out of doing it this way. The right panel now shows
real transients ŌĆö the camera actually overshoots and settles, the
photo bloom curve actually decays, markers actually catch up.
Comparison becomes about behaviour, not about end states.

But the recreation no longer runs at the same speed as the .mov.
Safari under live screen-recording load drops to ~25ŌĆō28 fps, vs the
SWF's effective 30 fps. So the recording's parser-time lags the
.mov's by a few percent, accumulating over 192 s into seconds of
end-of-trip drift. v10_strict made this drift visible by showing
blanks where no within-1-minute match existed (only 36 of 192 mov
frames had one). The pragmatic fix in v11+ was nearest-time matching
without a tolerance, accepting a few seconds of phase error as the
price of seeing actual transients.

The lesson, narrower than XV.6 below: when the question is *does
the recreation behave like the original*, capture has to preserve
the temporal evolution of both panels, not freeze either one. A
screen-recording rig with timestamp OCR is the cheap way; a
deterministic test-mode that ticks both at the same wall-clock rate
would be the principled way.

### XV.4 The photo bloom step-down

A specific bug worth recording. SWF photos enter at huge scale and
shrink fast: `scale = 3.5 + 9_000_000 / (|dt|+1) %`, where `dt` is
seconds from the photo's exact time. At dt=0 this is ~9M %, capped
in the SWF at `SCALE_CAP = 2500 %`. The intended dynamic is a hard
peak that decays over ~60 seconds back into the camera's normal
zoom range.

In porting, an early safety cap of `MAX_FINAL_SCALE = 100` was added
to keep `drawImage` from being called at absurd sizes during the
first one or two frames after activation. Because the natural curve
falls below 100 at around dt Ōēł 80 s, the cap was doing exactly the
wrong thing: photos sat at the cap for ~80 s, then suddenly
step-shrank when the natural curve crossed under the cap. Visible as
"the images jump from medium to small after settling", most obvious
in the dense Reykjav├Łk sequence.

Two fixes were considered. Remove the cap entirely, and let
`drawImage` handle absurd sizes via canvas viewport clipping (cheap
because the photo is clipped to a 500-px viewport before raster).
Or move the cap to where the natural curve would land at the first
post-activation tick, so the cap and the curve agree at that moment
and there is no perceptible step. The second was chosen
(`MAX_FINAL_SCALE = 80`, computed from median GPS gap ├Ś max camera
scale): it's defensive against pathological dt values that could
otherwise still ship megapixel rasters to the GPU.

A reminder that "safety caps" without checking whether the underlying
curve crosses them are silent visual bugs.

### XV.5 The marker-scale tweak (and what it admits)

Up to v12 the recreation rendered SWF design-unit shapes
byte-for-byte: octagon vertices at radius 0.20, scaled by the same
camera factor the SWF uses. In the side-by-side, the markers in the
right panel read consistently smaller than in the left.

The reason is the rig, not the recreation. The .mov was screen-
recorded at the SWF's native 478 ├Ś 478 px and is upsampled to
500 ├Ś 500 in the composite. The recreation is rendered at native
500 ├Ś 500 retina with no upsample. So every primitive in the .mov
panel gets a ~5 % size bonus from interpolation that the recreation
panel doesn't see. Photos compensate because they're raster and
their visual scale is dominated by the bloom math; line-art markers,
being design-unit primitives, show the difference cleanly.

v13 bumps the octagon design size by +15 % (slightly more than the
5 % rig delta, to account for stroke-width perception). It is the
only constant in the port that doesn't match the SWF byte-for-byte,
and it's marked in `index.html` as a side-by-side rig compensation,
not a fidelity fix. The live page (without the comparison panel)
should arguably revert to the SWF-faithful 0.20 radius. Recorded
here as an open question.

### XV.6 The "ground truth first" lesson

The harness above is the move that should have been made on day one.
Every iteration of code-reading, AS2 porting, and parameter-tweaking
between the first attempt and v9 was Claude trusting symbolic reasoning
over visual ground truth. Each tweak felt cheaper than the harness;
the cumulative cost of guessing was much higher than building it.

Captured to memory at `feedback_ground_truth_first.md` (in the
project's per-conversation memory directory) so the next session
opens with this loaded:

> When the user has a high-fidelity ground-truth artefact (a video,
> a screenshot, a render), build the synced comparison harness BEFORE
> the first iteration of porting. Treat it as the test harness, not
> as the polish step at the end.

This is the load-bearing process lesson of the whole session. Worth
more than any single technical finding.

## XVI. The artist-coder problem

A pattern Timo named after publication, worth recording for any
future archaeology. In every place where the SWF had an idiosyncratic
choice, Claude's first pass was the textbook implementation:

| What Even wrote in 2004 | What Claude built first |
|---|---|
| Parser eats one trkpt per animate frame | Linear time advance across the calendar |
| Empirical 30 fps (declared 120 in header) | 120 fps from the header |
| Second-order damped spring on time itself with `timeD *= 0.1` | First-order exponential ease on `dispT` |
| Photo `_alpha = timer` ramping to 100, with the CXForm `mul a=0.1` overridden by `loadMovie` | Photo opacity capped at literal CXForm value 0.10, then 0.35, then 0.55, then 0.85 |
| Bloom formula `3.5 + 9_000_000 / (dt + 1)` | Approximations of the curve before reading the AS2 |
| Camera spring constants `(1/40, 0.69)` and `(1/3, 0.59)` | Cleaner round numbers attempted first |
| 90├Ś CAMDRAWING vs 100├Ś EXTENTS scale ratio | Single scale factor for both states |
| `lat ├Ś -800`, `lon ├Ś 400` (a 2:1 squash, not a real projection) | Equirectangular with cosine correction at first |
| Hairline strokes from twip-stored line widths (1 screen pixel at any zoom) | Constant pixel widths that scaled with zoom |
| Trkpt-paced playback that elides overnight gaps for free | Linear advance that rendered overnight gaps as long static stretches |

Every entry in the right column is what a competent engineer in 2026
would write if handed the spec. None is what Even wrote in 2004.

The reason is not training-data dominance alone. The obvious choice is
also lower-risk for a system trained to defer to convention. A
constant of 0.69 looks like a typo to a system that rounds to clean
numbers. A parser that ignores timestamps looks like a bug to a system
that respects data. A CXForm of `mul a=0.1` looks like a hard cap to
a system that reads specs literally. A 120 fps header looks like the
truth to a system that trusts metadata. The cumulative effect: AI
risk-aversion at the small scale compounds into aesthetic cowardice at
the large scale. Median is the safe play. Median is also, by
definition, not interesting.

The corollary for software archaeology of artist-coder work: the first
pass will always be the version a competent contemporary engineer
would build. That is the wrong starting point if what you want is the
original. Each idiosyncratic choice has to be surfaced individually,
either by reading the bytecode hours and asking *"wait, why did they
do it this way?"*, or by someone in the loop with taste saying
*"no, that's not it"* and naming what is missing. The whole session
above is the record of Timo doing the second of those, twenty-four
times.

The practical consequence is that AI is not a substitute for the
human in this kind of work. It is a force-multiplier for a particular
kind of human: one who knows what the work felt like, can recognise
when the recreation has flattened, and is willing to keep saying so.
Without that person, an AI-led recovery flattens to the median
engineer's reading of the artefact. With them, it can recover the
artist's.
